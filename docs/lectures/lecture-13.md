## Empirical Motivation: Why Do Coefficients Change?

Consider a simple question: do Black and White Americans earn different
incomes? We can answer this with a regression using the General Social
Survey (GSS).

Recall that OLS with a binary regressor estimates a difference in group
means: regressing income on a Black/White indicator simply recovers the
average income gap between the two groups. Let’s run that regression,
and then add education as a control.

``` r
df_bw <- df |> filter(race %in% c("Black", "White"))

modelsummary(
  list(
    "Race only" = lm(income ~ race, data = df_bw),
    "+ Education" = lm(income ~ race + edu, data = df_bw)
  ),
  stars = TRUE,
  gof_map = c("nobs", "r.squared")
)
```

To understand what is happening, let’s build intuition with simulated
data where we know the true data-generating process.

### Illustration with simulated data

We consider two worlds. In both, income is generated by the same rule:
it depends on race and education. The only difference is whether race
and education are correlated with each other.

``` r
set.seed(42)
n <- 1000

# World 1: race and education are independent
sim1 <- tibble(
  black = rbinom(n, 1, 0.5),
  edu = rnorm(n, mean = 12, sd = 3), # same distribution for both groups
  income = 20000 - 3000 * black + 1500 * edu + rnorm(n, sd = 100)
)

# World 2: Black respondents have 2 fewer years of education on average
sim2 <- tibble(
  black = rbinom(n, 1, 0.5),
  edu = rnorm(n, mean = 12 - 2 * black, sd = 3), # correlated with race
  income = 20000 - 3000 * black + 1500 * edu + rnorm(n, sd = 100)
)
```

``` r
mods <- list(
  lm(income ~ black, data = sim1),
  lm(income ~ black + edu, data = sim1),
  lm(income ~ black, data = sim2),
  lm(income ~ black + edu, data = sim2)

modelsummary(
```

In World 1 (left panel), adding education leaves the race coefficient
essentially unchanged: because race and education are independent,
omitting education does not bias the race estimate.

In World 2 (right panel), the race coefficient shrinks substantially
when education is added. Because Black respondents have less education
on average, the race-only model conflates the direct effect of race with
the education penalty. Adding education separates the two.

**This is exactly the pattern we see in the GSS data.** Black
respondents have, on average, fewer years of education than White
respondents. The two variables are correlated, so adding education
shifts the race coefficient. Whether that shift reflects “explaining” or
“absorbing” the gap is a causal question — one that regression alone
cannot answer. At any rate, we can say that **education confounds the
relationship between race and income.**

**To remember.** When we add a control in an OLS regression, the
coefficient changes only if the control is correlated with the main
regressor. What that change reflects requires causal thinking — not just
more controls.

## Counterfactuals and the Fundamental Problem of Causal Inference

Causality is about understanding the effect of a **treatment** on an
**outcome**. Suppose we try to understand the impact of Advil (our
treatment) on body temperature (the outcome). Denote $Y_i$ the
temperature of subject $i$, and $T_i \in \{0,1\}$ whether subject $i$
took the treatment ($T_i = 1$) or not ($T_i = 0$). We can think of
$Y_i(T_i)$: the subject’s temperature given her treatment status. Let’s
say:

$$
Y_i(1) = 37^\circ C \quad \text{and} \quad Y_i(0) = 38^\circ C
$$

The **treatment effect** of Advil on her body temperature is
$Y_i(1) - Y_i(0) = -1^\circ C$.

The problem is that we can never observe both $Y_i(1)$ and $Y_i(0)$ for
the same subject. We can only observe one of them, depending on whether
the subject took Advil or not. The outcome we do not observe is called
the **counterfactual** outcome. This is the **fundamental problem of
causal inference**: we cannot observe the counterfactual outcome.

You may wonder why we do not observe the counterfactual outcome.
Specifically, at 3pm, you felt feverish and thus took an Advil. At 5pm,
you measured your temperature again and noticed it had dropped. You thus
concluded that Advil made your temperature drop. In reality, you made an
assumption here: **you assumed that the only thing that changed between
the two times you measured your temperature was taking the Advil.**
Based on this assumption, you concluded that the drop in temperature was
due to the Advil. But what if something else changed between the two
times you measured your temperature? For example, what if you also took
a nap, and that is what made your temperature drop? In that case, the
drop in temperature could possibly not be due to the Advil, but rather
to the nap. Here **the proper counterfactual is the following: what
would your temperature have been at 5pm had you not taken the Advil?**
We cannot observe this counterfactual, and thus we cannot be sure that
the drop in temperature was due to the Advil.

**To remember.** The individual treatment effect is
$\Delta_i = Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)$. We cannot estimate it because we observe
either $Y_i(1)$ or $Y_i(0)$, but never both. The outcome we do not
observe is the counterfactual. The fundamental problem of causal
inference is that we cannot observe the counterfactual.

## The Average Treatment Effect and Why Randomization Solves the Problem

### The intuition

Instead of trying to estimate the individual treatment effect
$\Delta_i = Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)$, we can try to estimate the **average
treatment effect (ATE)** across a population. To do so, we run a
**randomized controlled trial (RCT)**: we randomly assign subjects to
either a treatment group ($T_i = 1$) or a control group ($T_i = 0$).

Why does randomization help? Return to the Advil example. Suppose we
compare two individuals, $i$ and $j$. $i$ took Advil and $j$ did not. We
find $Y_i = 37^\circ C$ and $Y_j = 36.3^\circ C$. We might be tempted to
conclude that Advil *increases* temperature. But this comparison is
invalid for two reasons:

1.  **Selection**: $i$ took Advil *because* she was feverish. She
    selected into treatment. Her $Y_i(0)$ — what her temperature would
    have been without Advil — is $38^\circ C$, not $36.3^\circ C$.
2.  **Confounding**: $j$ may be younger and naturally have a lower
    resting temperature. The difference between $i$ and $j$ could be due
    to age, not Advil.

Randomizing assignment **across a large number of individuals** solves
both problems at once. Because we assign who takes Advil by a coin flip:

- There is no selection: treatment is not chosen by subjects, so those
  assigned to treatment are not systematically different from those
  assigned to control.
- There is no confounding: random assignment ensures that, on average,
  the treatment and control groups are identical in *all*
  characteristics — observed or unobserved. There are as many young/old
  and sick/healthy people in the treatment group as in the control
  group.

This is the key property of randomization: **treatment becomes
uncorrelated with everything else** — including potential outcomes. It
is as if we created two groups that are otherwise identical, and the
only difference between them is whether they received the treatment.

### The math

We want to estimate the ATE:

$$
ATE = \mathbb{E}[Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)]
$$

We never observe $Y_i(1)$ and $Y_i(0)$ for the same individual
simultaneously. But we can try to estimate $\mathbb{E}[Y_i(1)]$ and
$\mathbb{E}[Y_i(0)]$ separately, using the treated and control groups
respectively.

The challenge is that the treated group is not the whole population: it
is the subgroup with $T_i = 1$. So what we observe is
$\mathbb{E}[Y_i(1) \mid T_i = 1]$, not $\mathbb{E}[Y_i(1)]$. In general,
these two quantities are different — the treated group is a selected
sample. In the Advil example, the treated group consists mainly of sick
people, so $\mathbb{E}[Y_i(1) \mid T_i = 1] > \mathbb{E}[Y_i(1)]$.

With **random assignment**, however, treatment $T_i$ is independent of
potential outcomes $(Y_i(1), Y_i(0))$:

$$
T_i \perp (Y_i(1), Y_i(0))
$$

This is precisely the mathematical statement that treatment is
uncorrelated with everything. And it has a decisive consequence:
conditioning on $T_i$ tells us nothing about $Y_i(1)$ or $Y_i(0)$, so:

$$
\mathbb{E}[Y_i(1) \mid T_i = 1] = \mathbb{E}[Y_i(1)]
\qquad \text{and} \qquad
\mathbb{E}[Y_i(0) \mid T_i = 0] = \mathbb{E}[Y_i(0)]
$$

The treated group is a representative sample of the population for
$Y_i(1)$, and the control group is a representative sample for $Y_i(0)$.
*It is only because of this independence that* we can write:

$$
\mathbb{E}[Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)] = \mathbb{E}[Y_i(1)] - \mathbb{E}[Y_i(0)] = \mathbb{E}[Y_i(1) \mid T_i = 1] - \mathbb{E}[Y_i(0) \mid T_i = 0]
$$

### From expectations to sample averages

By the **Law of Large Numbers**, with enough observations, sample
averages converge to population expectations:

$$
\bar{Y}_1 = \frac{1}{n_1}\sum_{i:\, T_i=1} Y_i \approx \mathbb{E}[Y_i(1) \mid T_i = 1] = \mathbb{E}[Y_i(1)]
$$

Putting it all together:

$$
\widehat{ATE} = \bar{Y}_1 - \bar{Y}_0 \approx \mathbb{E}[Y_i(1)] - \mathbb{E}[Y_i(0)] = ATE
$$

**The difference in sample means is a valid estimator of the ATE — only
because of random assignment.**

In our Advil example: suppose the experiment yields
$\bar{Y}_1 = 37^\circ C$ in the treatment group and
$\bar{Y}_0 = 37.5^\circ C$ in the control group. The $37.5^\circ C$
reflects the fact that the control group contains both healthy people
(temperature $\approx 37^\circ C$) and feverish people (temperature
$\approx 38^\circ C$). The treatment group, being equally mixed by
random assignment, would also have averaged $37.5^\circ C$ without the
drug — but Advil brought feverish subjects down to $37^\circ C$. The
estimated ATE is $37 - 37.5 = -0.5^\circ C$.

**To remember.** Randomization makes treatment uncorrelated with
potential outcomes: $T_i \perp (Y_i(1), Y_i(0))$. This is what allows us
to use the treated group to estimate $\mathbb{E}[Y_i(1)]$ and the
control group to estimate $\mathbb{E}[Y_i(0)]$. The difference in sample
means then estimates the ATE — but only under random assignment.

## Heterogeneous Treatment Effects

The ATE averages the treatment effect across the entire population. But
treatment effects often vary across subgroups — we say they are
**heterogeneous**.

Return to the Advil example. Suppose we estimate the ATE separately for
healthy and sick people:

- For **healthy** people: $\bar{Y}_1 = 37^\circ C$ and
  $\bar{Y}_0 = 37^\circ C$, so $ATE = 0$.
- For **sick** people: $\bar{Y}_1 = 37^\circ C$ and
  $\bar{Y}_0 = 38^\circ C$, so $ATE = -1^\circ C$.

Advil works for sick people, not for healthy people. The overall ATE
pools these two groups together and gives a number somewhere in between
— which is informative for average policy, but masks who actually
benefits.

Knowing *who* gets treated matters. If a drug is only assigned to
healthy people, the overall ATE will understate the effect for those who
actually need it.

**To remember.** Treatment effects can vary across subgroups. We can
estimate subgroup ATEs by repeating the randomization logic within each
group, as long as we measure the relevant characteristics before
treatment assignment.

## Application: The Bertrand Experiment

### Context

Bertrand & Mullainathan (2004) ran a field experiment to measure racial
discrimination in the labor market. They sent fictitious résumés to real
job postings in Boston and Chicago. The résumés were otherwise
identical, but the **name** on the résumé was randomly assigned to
signal race: stereotypically Black names (Lakisha, Jamal) or White names
(Emily, Greg). The outcome was whether the employer called back.

This is a genuine experiment: the name — and thus the race signal — was
randomly assigned. So:

$$
T_i \perp (Y_i(1), Y_i(0))
$$

The difference in callback rates between groups is therefore a valid
causal estimate of the effect of race on employer callbacks.

### Analysis

Recall that OLS with a binary regressor estimates a difference in group
means. Here `race` is binary (black/white), so regressing `call` on
`race` directly recovers
$\bar{Y}_{\text{white}} - \bar{Y}_{\text{black}}$ — the estimated ATE.

any other characteristic.

### Connecting back to the GSS

In the GSS analysis, we saw that the Black–White income gap shrinks once
we control for education. But we could not determine whether this
reflected causation or confounding. Here, we *can* make a causal claim:
the résumés were identical except for the name, so the gap in callbacks
can only be due to the name — i.e., to the perceived race of the
applicant.

This illustrates the power of random assignment. Without it (as in the
GSS), we are always unsure whether our estimate reflects causation or
the fact that treated and untreated individuals differ in unobserved
ways.

**To remember.** The Bertrand experiment estimates the causal effect of
perceived race on employer callbacks. Random assignment of names
guarantees that $\bar{Y}_1 - \bar{Y}_0$ is a valid ATE estimate. The
result — a large, significant callback gap — provides direct evidence of
racial discrimination in hiring.
